How this auto loan calculator works

The amount you finance is the vehicle price plus sales tax, minus your down payment and any trade-in. That financed amount, your interest rate, and the term feed the standard amortization formula to produce your monthly payment. Auto loans run on much shorter terms than mortgages, so total interest is smaller — but the same rules apply: early payments are interest-heavy, later ones go to principal. The calculator also shows total interest and the all-in cost, which is the number that matters when comparing offers.

The two biggest levers are the down payment and the term. A larger down payment shrinks the financed amount, lowering both the payment and total interest, and protects you from being "upside down" (owing more than the car is worth). A longer term makes the monthly payment look smaller but quietly increases total interest. Try dropping the term from 72 to 48 months and watch total interest fall.

Tips before you sign

Get pre-approved from your bank or credit union before the dealership so you have a rate to beat, negotiate the vehicle price separately from financing, and always compare total cost — not just the monthly payment. For deeper loan math, see our guides.

Frequently asked questions

How is a car payment calculated?
From the amount financed (price + tax − down payment − trade-in), the monthly rate, and the number of months.
Is a longer auto loan term cheaper?
No — it lowers the monthly payment but raises total interest and the risk of owing more than the car is worth.
