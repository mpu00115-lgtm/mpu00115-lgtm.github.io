How this mortgage calculator works

This calculator uses the standard amortization formula to turn three simple inputs — your loan amount, interest rate, and term — into a precise monthly payment. Each month, part of that payment covers interest on the remaining balance and the rest reduces your principal. Early in the loan most of the payment is interest; over time the balance shrinks and more of every dollar goes to principal. The full schedule above shows that shift month by month, so you can see exactly when you cross the halfway point and how much interest the loan really costs.

The extra payment field is where most homeowners find real savings. Because anything above the scheduled amount is applied straight to principal, even a small recurring extra payment compounds: it lowers the balance that interest is charged on, which lowers next month's interest, and so on. On a typical 30-year loan, an extra $100–$200 a month can remove several years and tens of thousands of dollars in interest. Try changing the extra payment and watch the payoff time and total interest update.

Tips to lower your total mortgage cost

Shopping rates from multiple lenders, improving your credit score before applying, choosing a shorter term if you can afford the higher monthly payment, and making biweekly instead of monthly payments are the four levers with the biggest impact. Use the calculator to compare scenarios side by side before you commit.

Frequently asked questions

How is my monthly mortgage payment calculated?
It uses the amortization formula based on your loan amount, the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and the total number of monthly payments (years × 12).
What is an amortization schedule?
A month-by-month table showing how each payment splits between interest and principal, plus the remaining balance until the loan reaches zero.
How do extra payments shorten my mortgage?
Extra money goes entirely to principal, so the balance — and the interest charged on it — falls faster, often cutting years off the loan.
