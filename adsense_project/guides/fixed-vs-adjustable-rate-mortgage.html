Fixed vs Adjustable-Rate Mortgage: Which Is Better in 2026?

By the PlainFigures Editorial Team · Published June 7, 2026 · Reviewed against current 2026 lending practices

Choosing between a fixed-rate mortgage and an adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) is one of the first big forks in the home-buying road, and the wrong choice can quietly cost you thousands. The two products solve different problems: one buys you certainty, the other buys you a lower starting payment in exchange for future uncertainty. Neither is universally "better" — the right answer depends almost entirely on how long you plan to keep the loan and how much payment risk you can stomach. This guide breaks down exactly how each works, the traps that catch borrowers, and a simple framework for deciding.

How a Fixed-Rate Mortgage Works

With a fixed-rate loan, your interest rate is locked for the entire term — typically 15 or 30 years. The principal-and-interest portion of your payment never changes, no matter what happens to the broader economy. That predictability is the product's whole appeal: you can budget for decades knowing the number won't move. The trade-off is that lenders charge a premium for taking on that long-term risk, so the starting rate is usually higher than an ARM's introductory rate. If rates fall significantly later, you don't benefit automatically — you'd have to refinance.

How an Adjustable-Rate Mortgage Works

An ARM starts with a fixed "teaser" period at a lower rate, then adjusts periodically. The common shorthand — like 5/1, 7/1, or 10/1 — tells you two things: the first number is how many years the rate stays fixed, and the second is how often it adjusts afterward (a "1" means once a year). When the adjustable period begins, your rate resets to a benchmark index plus a fixed margin, subject to caps that limit how much it can jump at each adjustment and over the life of the loan. The lower intro rate means a smaller starting payment — but you're betting on where rates will be years from now.

The Real Risk Most Borrowers Underestimate

The danger of an ARM isn't the adjustment itself — it's payment shock combined with being unable to escape. People often plan to "just refinance or sell before the rate adjusts." But life intervenes: home values can dip, leaving you with too little equity to refinance affordably; your income or credit can change, making you ineligible; or selling may not be practical when the deadline arrives. If you're still in the loan when it adjusts upward, your payment can rise meaningfully, and the caps only limit the damage, they don't prevent it. An ARM is safest when your exit is genuinely near-certain, not merely hoped for.

When a Fixed Rate Wins

Choose fixed if you plan to stay in the home long term, if a higher future payment would strain your budget, or if you simply value sleeping well over squeezing out a slightly lower starting rate. For most owner-occupants buying a "forever-ish" home, the certainty is worth the premium. A fixed rate also pairs naturally with a strategy of making extra principal payments, because your stable payment makes the extra contribution easy to plan.

When an ARM Can Make Sense

An ARM can be the smarter pick in a few specific situations: you're confident you'll move within the fixed period (a job relocation, a starter home you'll outgrow), the rate gap between fixed and ARM is unusually wide, or you expect rates to fall and intend to refinance. In those cases, you pocket real savings during the intro years. The key is honesty about your timeline — an ARM rewards certainty about the future and punishes wishful thinking.

The Bottom Line

If your timeline is long or uncertain, a fixed-rate mortgage's predictability is almost always worth the slightly higher rate. An ARM is a precision tool: brilliant when you're genuinely certain you'll exit during the intro period, dangerous when "I'll probably refinance" is doing the heavy lifting. Before deciding, model both in the mortgage calculator — run the fixed rate, then run the ARM's intro rate and a realistic post-reset rate — and look at the total interest under each scenario, not just the comfortable first-year payment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a fixed or adjustable-rate mortgage better?
Fixed is better for predictable, long-term plans; an ARM can win if you're certain you'll sell or refinance before it adjusts and want a lower starting rate.
What does 5/1 ARM mean?
The rate is fixed for 5 years, then adjusts once per year based on an index plus a margin, within caps.
Can you refinance an ARM to a fixed rate?
Yes — many borrowers do so before the adjustable period starts, though it means qualifying again and paying closing costs.
