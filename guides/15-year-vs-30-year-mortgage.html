15-Year vs 30-Year Mortgage: Which Actually Saves More?

By the PlainFigures Editorial Team · Published June 7, 2026 · Reviewed against current 2026 lending practices

The choice between a 15-year and a 30-year mortgage is really a choice between two financial philosophies: pay less interest overall, or keep more cash flexible each month. Both are defensible, and the "right" answer depends less on the math alone than on how the math fits the rest of your life. This guide lays out the real differences — in monthly payment, total interest, equity, and flexibility — and gives you a simple framework so you can decide with confidence instead of guessing.

The Core Trade-Off

A 15-year mortgage compresses repayment into half the time. That does two powerful things: it slashes the total interest you pay, and it builds equity dramatically faster because more of every payment goes to principal from day one. Lenders also reward the shorter commitment with a slightly lower interest rate. The catch is the monthly payment, which is meaningfully higher — usually around 40–50% more than the same loan over 30 years — because you're squeezing the same principal into fewer payments.

A 30-year mortgage flips the priorities. The lower required payment frees up monthly cash flow that you can direct toward retirement accounts, an emergency fund, or simply a more comfortable life. The price is steep over time: you pay far more total interest, and you build equity slowly in the early years. Neither approach is "wrong" — they optimize for different things.

The headline that often sells people on a 15-year loan is total interest: cutting the term roughly in half can save six figures over the life of a large loan. That number is real and powerful — but it only matters if the higher payment doesn't force you to underfund retirement or carry high-interest debt elsewhere.

The "Best of Both" Strategy

There's a third option many people overlook: take the 30-year loan, but voluntarily pay it like a 15-year by adding extra to principal each month. This gives you the interest savings of an aggressive payoff with a safety valve — if you lose income or hit an emergency, you can drop back to the lower required payment without penalty. The trade-off is a slightly higher interest rate than a true 15-year loan, and the discipline required to actually make the extra payments. For many borrowers, that flexibility is worth the small rate premium. You can model exactly how extra payments shrink the timeline using the extra-payment field in our mortgage calculator.

How to Decide

Lean toward a 15-year loan if the higher payment still leaves you fully funding retirement, you have a solid emergency cushion, you carry no high-interest debt, and you value being mortgage-free sooner. Lean toward a 30-year loan if a lower payment lets you invest the difference, you want a wider safety margin, or you're early in your career with rising income expected. And if you want the interest savings without locking yourself in, choose the 30-year and pay extra by choice. The worst outcome is stretching for a 15-year payment so tight that one bad month forces you into credit-card debt — that erases the interest savings and then some.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a 15-year mortgage always better than a 30-year?
No. It saves interest and builds equity faster, but the higher payment reduces flexibility. The best choice depends on your full financial picture.
Can I pay off a 30-year mortgage like a 15-year?
Yes — take the 30-year loan and add extra to principal each month. You get most of the savings while keeping the option to fall back to the lower payment.
Why is the interest rate lower on a 15-year mortgage?
Lenders take on less long-term risk with a shorter term, so they usually offer a slightly lower rate.
