What Credit Score Do You Need to Buy a House?

By the PlainFigures Editorial Team · Published June 7, 2026 · Reviewed against current 2026 lending practices

People tend to ask "what's the minimum credit score to buy a house?" — but that's the wrong question. There's almost always some loan available at a given score; the real issue is what that score does to your interest rate, and therefore to the total cost of the home. A few dozen points can be the difference between a comfortable payment and tens of thousands in extra interest. This guide explains the score lenders actually look for, how your number translates into a rate, and the concrete steps that move your score before you apply.

The Scores Lenders Look For

Different loan programs set different bars. As a general guide in 2026:

Loan typeTypical score floorNotes
Conventional~620+Best rates usually need 740+
FHA (government-backed)Lower, often ~580+Allows smaller down payments
Jumbo / premiumHigher, often 700+Stricter for large loans

These are not hard universal rules — each lender layers its own requirements on top, considering your down payment, debt-to-income ratio, and income stability. But the pattern holds everywhere: meeting the minimum gets you a loan; a strong score gets you a good one.

Why a Higher Score Saves So Much

Lenders price risk. A higher score signals you're more likely to repay, so they charge you a lower interest rate. Because a mortgage is enormous and runs for decades, even a small rate difference compounds into a large dollar figure. The gap between a "fair" score and an "excellent" one can raise your rate by a meaningful fraction of a percent — and on a typical 30-year loan, that can add tens of thousands of dollars in total interest, plus a higher monthly payment for the entire term. Improving your score isn't cosmetic; it's one of the highest-return financial moves available before you buy.

How to Raise Your Score Before Applying

The good news is that the biggest levers are within your control, and several work within a few billing cycles:

  • Pay every bill on time. Payment history is the single largest factor. One missed payment can sting for a long time, so automate minimums.
  • Lower your credit utilization. Paying down credit-card balances so you use a smaller share of your limits is one of the fastest ways to lift a score. Aim well below your limit, not just under it.
  • Don't open new accounts right before applying. New credit triggers hard inquiries and lowers your average account age — both can dip your score at the worst moment.
  • Check your reports for errors. Mistakes are common. Dispute inaccuracies, because a single wrongly reported late payment can cost you real money on your rate.
  • Keep old accounts open. Length of credit history helps; closing your oldest card can quietly hurt.

If your score is close to a threshold, even a month or two of focused effort can nudge you into a better rate tier — which is exactly when the mortgage calculator becomes useful for seeing the payoff in real dollars.

The Bottom Line

Don't aim for the minimum; aim for the best score you can reasonably reach before applying. Lock in on-time payments, drive down card balances, leave your credit profile stable in the months before you shop, and fix any report errors.
