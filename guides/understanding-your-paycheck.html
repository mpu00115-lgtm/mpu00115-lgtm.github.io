Understanding Your Paycheck: Where Your Money Actually Goes

By the PlainFigures Editorial Team · Published June 7, 2026 · Reviewed against current 2026 payroll rules

Almost everyone has had the same small shock: you negotiate a salary, then the first paycheck arrives noticeably smaller than you expected. The gap between the number on your offer letter and the number in your bank account is filled by a handful of deductions — some required by law, some chosen by you. Once you understand each line, your pay stub stops being a mystery and becomes a tool you can actually optimize. This guide walks through every piece, in plain English.

Gross Pay vs Net Pay

Gross pay is your salary before anything is removed — the headline figure. Net pay, or take-home pay, is what actually reaches you after every deduction. The journey from one to the other happens in a specific order, and understanding that order is the key to reading your stub. Pre-tax deductions come out first, lowering the income that gets taxed; then the taxes are calculated; what survives is your net pay.

The Deductions, Line by Line

LineWhat it is
Federal income taxTax on your income using progressive brackets and the standard deduction. Set by your W-4.
Social Security6.2% of wages up to an annual wage base — part of FICA.
Medicare1.45% of all wages, no cap — the other part of FICA.
State income taxVaries widely; some states have none, others charge progressive rates.
401(k) / retirementPre-tax (traditional) contributions that lower taxable income now.
Health insuranceYour share of premiums, often pre-tax, reducing taxable income too.

The two FICA lines confuse people most. They aren't income tax — they fund specific programs, your employer pays a matching amount you never see, and Social Security stops being withheld once you pass the annual wage base. Medicare, by contrast, applies to every dollar.

The Power of Pre-Tax Deductions

Here's the part worth acting on: pre-tax deductions like a traditional 401(k) or HSA reduce the income that federal and state taxes are calculated on. That means contributing doesn't cost you dollar-for-dollar — a portion of what you set aside is money that would have gone to taxes anyway. This is why raising your 401(k) contribution feels less painful than the number suggests, and why financial advisors push so hard to at least capture any employer match. Use the 401(k) field in the paycheck calculator to see exactly how your take-home changes when you contribute more — the drop is almost always smaller than the amount saved.

How to Adjust Your Withholding

If you get a big tax refund every year, you essentially gave the government an interest-free loan — your withholding is too high. If you owe a lot in April, it's too low. You control this with your W-4 form, which you can update with your employer anytime. The goal isn't a giant refund; it's accuracy, so more of your money stays in each paycheck where it can work for you. Check the glossary for any terms here that are new, and model changes before you submit a new W-4.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my take-home pay so much lower than my salary?
Federal tax, Social Security and Medicare (FICA), state tax, and pre-tax items like 401(k) and insurance all come out before you're paid.
What is FICA on my pay stub?
The payroll tax funding Social Security (6.2% up to a wage base) and Medicare (1.45%), withheld every paycheck and matched by your employer.
Do 401(k) contributions lower my taxes?
Traditional 401(k) contributions are pre-tax, lowering taxable income now; you pay tax later when you withdraw.
