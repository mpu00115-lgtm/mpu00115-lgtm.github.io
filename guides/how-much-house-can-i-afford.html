How Much House Can I Afford? A Realistic 2026 Guide

By the PlainFigures Editorial Team · Published June 7, 2026 · Reviewed against current 2026 lending practices

"How much house can I afford?" is the question that should come before you ever browse listings — yet most people answer it backward, falling in love with a home first and stretching the budget to fit. The honest answer has two layers: the amount a lender will approve, and the amount you can comfortably carry without sacrificing the rest of your life. Those two numbers are rarely the same, and the gap between them is where financial stress lives. This guide shows you how to find your real number using a simple rule, your true monthly budget, and the ownership costs that quietly add up.

Start With the 28/36 Rule

The most widely used affordability guideline is the 28/36 rule. It says your housing costs should stay at or below 28% of your gross monthly income, and your total debt payments — housing plus car loans, student loans, and minimum credit-card payments — should stay at or below 36%. Lenders use a version of these debt-to-income ratios to decide what you qualify for. They're a sensible ceiling, but treat 28/36 as a maximum, not a target. Many financially comfortable owners deliberately stay well below it.

Work Backward From a Monthly Payment

The most practical method flips the usual process. Instead of asking "what price home can I get," ask "what monthly payment can I comfortably live with?" Decide on a number that leaves room for saving and living — then plug it into a calculator to find the loan amount that payment supports at current rates. This anchors your search to your actual budget rather than a lender's maximum, and it instantly shows how rate changes move your buying power.

The Costs People Forget (PITI and Beyond)

A mortgage payment is more than principal and interest. The full monthly figure — often called PITI — adds property taxes and insurance, and if your down payment is under 20%, PMI too. Beyond the payment, ownership brings costs renting never did: maintenance and repairs (a common rule of thumb is budgeting around 1% of the home's value per year), HOA dues in some communities, higher utility bills in a larger space, and closing costs upfront. Affordability that ignores these is fiction. Build them into your number from the start.

Why You Usually Shouldn't Borrow the Maximum

Lenders approve based on ratios and your credit profile, not on whether you want to travel, invest, or weather a job loss. Borrowing the full approved amount often leaves a thin margin — and a thin margin turns ordinary surprises (a car repair, a medical bill, a few months of reduced income) into crises. Buying somewhat below your maximum preserves the flexibility that makes ownership a source of stability rather than stress. The goal isn't the biggest house you can get; it's the house that lets the rest of your financial life keep working.

The Bottom Line

Your true affordability is the smaller of two numbers: what the lender approves and what your real budget — including taxes, insurance, maintenance, and a cushion — can comfortably absorb. Use the 28/36 rule as a ceiling, work backward from a payment you're happy with, and resist the pull of the maximum approval. Then confirm the math in the mortgage calculator so the home you choose strengthens your finances instead of straining them.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 28/36 rule?
Keep housing costs at or below 28% of gross monthly income and total debt at or below 36%.
How much income do I need to buy a house?
It depends on price, down payment, rate, and other debts. Work backward: choose a comfortable payment, then find the loan it supports.
Should I borrow the maximum the lender approves?
Usually not — the maximum often leaves too little room for savings, emergencies, and the real costs of owning.
