How Much Should You Save for Retirement?

By the PlainFigures Editorial Team · Published June 7, 2026 · Reviewed against current 2026 best practices

"How much do I need to retire?" feels like an impossibly large question, which is exactly why so many people avoid answering it. But you don't need a perfect crystal ball — you need a reasonable target and a habit. This guide gives you both: simple savings benchmarks by age, the reason starting early matters so much, and a withdrawal rule that turns a savings number into a retirement paycheck. The goal is to replace vague anxiety with a number you can plan around.

Two Simple Benchmarks

Financial planners often use two rules of thumb that work together. The first is a savings rate: aim to put away around 15% of your income each year, including any employer match. The second is a multiple of salary by certain ages — a widely cited progression looks like this:

AgeTarget saved (× salary)
30~1×
40~3×
50~6×
60~8×
67 (retirement)~10×

These are guideposts, not commandments. If you're behind, they show how much ground to make up; if you're ahead, they're reassurance. Your personal number shifts with how much you plan to spend, when you retire, and other income like Social Security or a pension.

Why Starting Early Beats Saving More

The single most important variable isn't how much you earn — it's time. Because of compounding, money invested in your twenties has decades to grow, and the growth itself starts generating growth. A modest contribution started early often ends up larger than a much bigger contribution started fifteen years later. This is the most encouraging fact in personal finance: you don't have to be wealthy to retire comfortably, but you do have to start. Turning Savings Into Income: The 4% Rule

Once you have a target balance, how do you know if it's enough? A common starting point is the 4% rule: in your first year of retirement, you withdraw about 4% of your savings, then adjust that dollar amount for inflation each year. Historically, that pace gave a reasonable chance of the money lasting roughly 30 years. Flipped around, it's a quick way to size your goal: multiply the annual income you want from savings by 25. Want $40,000 a year from your portfolio? That implies a target near $1,000,000. The rule is a simplification — real markets, taxes, and lifespans vary — but it converts an abstract goal into a concrete one.

What to Do If You're Behind

If these numbers feel out of reach, don't despair — adjust the levers you control. Increase your savings rate gradually (even 1% a year adds up), capture the full employer match before anything else, use catch-up contributions if you're over 50, and consider working a little longer, which both adds savings and shortens the years your money must cover. Most importantly, start now: the worst version of this plan is the one you keep postponing. Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I save for retirement?
A common guideline is ~15% of income per year, aiming for roughly 10× your final salary by retirement — adjusted for your spending and other income.
What is the 4% rule?
A guideline that withdrawing about 4% of savings in year one (then adjusting for inflation) gives a reasonable chance the money lasts ~30 years.
Is it too late to start saving for retirement?
Rarely. Starting later means saving more and possibly working longer, but consistent contributions still build meaningful savings.
