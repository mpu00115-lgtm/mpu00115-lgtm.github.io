How credit card payoff works

Each month, interest is charged on your remaining balance at your card's monthly rate (APR ÷ 12). Your payment first covers that interest, and only what's left chips away at the principal. That's why a balance that looks small can take years to clear: when the payment barely exceeds the interest, almost nothing reduces what you owe. The fastest way to break the cycle is to pay more than the minimum — every extra dollar goes straight to principal, which lowers next month's interest, which frees up even more of the following payment. The effect compounds in your favor.

Use the extra per month field to test this. Adding even $50 on top of your regular payment often cuts months off the timeline and saves hundreds in interest, because you're attacking the principal directly. The higher your APR, the more dramatic the savings from paying extra.

Strategies to pay off faster

Beyond paying extra, two proven methods help: the avalanche (pay the highest-APR card first to minimize total interest) and the snowball (pay the smallest balance first for quick motivating wins). A balance transfer to a 0% intro-APR card can also pause interest, but watch for transfer fees and the rate after the promo ends. For the underlying math, see our money guides.

Frequently asked questions

How long will it take to pay off my credit card?
It depends on balance, APR, and payment. A higher monthly payment shortens the timeline and sharply cuts total interest.
Why does paying only the minimum cost so much?
Minimums barely exceed the interest, so the balance shrinks slowly and you pay interest for years.
