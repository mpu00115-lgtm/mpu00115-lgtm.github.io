How this paycheck calculator works

Your paycheck starts with gross pay, but several deductions come out before the money reaches you. First, pre-tax contributions like a traditional 401(k) are subtracted, which also lowers your taxable income. Then federal income tax is applied using 2026 tax brackets and the standard deduction for your filing status. On top of that, FICA taxes fund Social Security (6.2% up to the annual wage base) and Medicare (1.45% with no cap). Finally, most states levy their own income tax, entered here as a flat rate for a quick estimate. What remains is your take-home — the number that actually hits your bank account.

The single biggest lever most workers control is the 401(k) percentage. Because those dollars come out before federal and state tax, raising your contribution lowers your taxable income today while building retirement savings. Try bumping the 401(k) from 5% to 10% and watch how much smaller the federal-tax line becomes — the net cost to your paycheck is less than the amount you save.

Why your real paycheck may differ

This is an estimate. It does not account for additional withholdings (W-4 extra amounts), local city taxes, health-insurance premiums, HSA/FSA deductions, or supplemental wages like bonuses, which are taxed differently. Use it to understand the shape of your paycheck and to compare scenarios, then confirm exact figures with your employer's payroll or a tax professional.

Frequently asked questions

How is take-home pay calculated?
Gross pay minus pre-tax deductions (like 401k), then federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, and state tax.
What is the difference between gross and net pay?
Gross is your pay before deductions; net (take-home) is what's left after taxes and deductions.
