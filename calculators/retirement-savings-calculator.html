How this retirement calculator works

Each year, your balance earns the expected return, and your monthly contributions are added on top. Over decades, the returns themselves start earning returns — this is compounding, and it's why the green "growth" portion of the chart eventually dwarfs what you actually contributed. The earlier you start, the more years compounding has to work, which is why a 25-year-old saving modestly often ends up ahead of a 40-year-old saving aggressively.

The chart shows your projected balance year by year. Notice how the curve bends upward rather than rising in a straight line — that acceleration is compounding. Try changing the annual return or monthly contribution and watch how dramatically small differences widen over 30+ years. A single percentage point of return can mean six figures by retirement.

Important assumptions

This projection assumes a steady annual return, which real markets never deliver — actual returns swing year to year, and a major downturn near retirement matters more than the average suggests. It also ignores inflation (a dollar at 65 buys less than today), taxes, and fees. Treat the result as a planning estimate to compare scenarios, not a promise. Frequently asked questions

How much will my retirement savings grow?
It depends on your starting balance, contributions, return, and years invested. Compounding means most of the final balance often comes from growth.
What return should I assume?
A common long-term assumption is 6–7% before inflation for a diversified, stock-heavy portfolio — but returns vary and are never guaranteed.
